Police Violence In the U.S

Within the last decade, there have been many prominent social justice movements against police brutality. It’s easy to understand why—police officers in the United States fatally kill people every day 1. In some cases, they kill more US citizens in a day than police officers in other countries do in years 2.

Many wonder why the United States has seen so many fathers, mothers, and children lost to police violence. This leads civillians to ask—who is most at risk of police brutality? We decided to look at the demographics of this problem by analyzing a data set created by the Washington Post, which is currently tracking fatal shootings of civillians by police officers since January 1st of 2015 3.

# Set colors for points on the interactive map
pal <- colorFactor(c("blue", "grey", "red"), domain = 
                     c("armed", "unarmed", "not yet known"))

# Create the interactive map
# Add six sets of circle markers based on the race of the victim
# Make checkboxes that let viewers click to display police shootings based on the race of the victim
# Set the view of the interactive map to snow the center of the 48 contiguous U.S. states

When we look at this interactive map of all the fatal police shootings since January 1st of 2015, we can see that fatal police shootings occur everywhere across the United States, with clusters around highly populated areas. The points are colored based on whether or not the victim was armed, to reflect how police have killed unarmed civillians4.

One wonders whether these shootings were justified on the part of the officer. Perhaps America has more fatal police shootings than other countries because there are more incidences of violence perpetrated by armed people? The media has attempted to bring attention to instances of fatal police shootings, but data on fatal police shootings has been acknowledged as incomplete by the FBI and CDC, who both track fatal police shootings5.
